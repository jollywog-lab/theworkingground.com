Colour Theory Principles

Wada's palettes work because they balance multiple colour relationships simultaneously. Understanding these principles helps explain why "wrong" combinations feel right.

Temperature Contrast
Warm colours (reds, oranges, yellows) paired with cool colours (blues, greens, purples). The tension creates visual interest while shared value or saturation maintains harmony.
Complementary Harmony
Colours opposite on the colour wheel (orange/blue, red/green, yellow/purple). Pure complements clash; muted or shifted versions harmonise — Wada's preferred approach.
Value Structure
Distribution of light, medium, and dark tones. Varied values create depth. Wada frequently anchors palettes with black or near-black to give lighter colours room to breathe.
Saturation Contrast
Vibrant colours anchored by muted tones. A saturated hue alongside neutrals becomes sophisticated rather than garish. The muted colours provide visual rest.
Tonal Relationships
Colours sharing undertones despite different hues — all slightly warm, all slightly greyed. Creates cohesion even when hues differ considerably.
Cultural Juxtaposition
Wada drew from Japanese dye traditions and natural observation. These pairings carry cultural memory, making them feel inevitable rather than arbitrary.
About Sanzo Wada

Sanzo Wada (1883–1967) was a Japanese artist, costume designer, and professor who compiled the Dictionary of Colour Combinations from his six-volume Haishoku Soukan, originally published in the 1930s for kimono and fashion design. Seigensha published the collected edition in 2010. The work contains 348 palettes grounded in natural observation and cultural memory.

His approach prioritises surprising harmony over safe gradation. Where algorithmic generators produce colours that share obvious relationships, Wada juxtaposes temperatures, saturations, and cultural contexts to create combinations that shouldn't work but do.

All colour values are derived from Wada's original CMYK specifications, converted to hex via the open-source dataset compiled by Dain M. Blodorn Kim. Combination numbers reference Wada's original catalogue. Click any swatch to copy its hex code.
