Colour Theory Principles

Wada's palettes work because they balance multiple colour relationships simultaneously.

Temperature Contrast
Warm colours paired with cool colours. The tension creates visual interest while shared value or saturation maintains harmony.
Complementary Harmony
Colours opposite on the colour wheel. Pure complements clash; muted versions harmonise — Wada's preferred approach.
Value Structure
Distribution of light, medium, and dark tones. Wada frequently anchors palettes with black or near-black.
Saturation Contrast
Vibrant colours anchored by muted tones. The muted colours provide visual rest while the vibrant colour provides energy.
Tonal Relationships
Colours sharing undertones despite different hues — all slightly warm, all slightly greyed. Creates cohesion even when hues differ.
Cultural Juxtaposition
Wada drew from Japanese dye traditions and natural observation. These pairings carry cultural memory, making them feel inevitable.
About Sanzo Wada

Sanzo Wada (1883–1967) was a Japanese artist, costume designer, and professor who compiled the Dictionary of Colour Combinations from his six-volume Haishoku Soukan, originally published in the 1930s for kimono and fashion design.

All colour values are derived from Wada's original CMYK specifications, converted to hex via the open-source dataset compiled by Dain M. Blodorn Kim. Combination numbers reference Wada's original catalogue. Click any swatch to copy its hex code.
